Learning Labs Are An Innovative Learning Format

Core concept:

  • Learn and teach: Students learn a lesson from a more advanced student and then teach it to a more beginning student.
  • Curriculum: Peer-teachable mini-lessons
  • An app: Track your progress and pair up with other students

Example:

  1. Alita walks into a salsa learning lab.
  2. The instructor pairs Alita to learn the first lesson from Petra.
  3. Petra shows Alita how to do the basic step.
  4. Now that Alita knows it, she can teach it to Hector walking fresh into the door.
  5. Alita checks her app and finds the next mentor to learn the second lesson from.

Benefits For Students

#1 Go at your own pace. Start anytime.

All you need is a mentoring student. You pick the next lesson.

#2 Hands-on instruction, guidance, and feedback

Instructors in a group class have many students. You have the full attention of your mentoring student to be shown instruction hands-on, ask questions, and get feedback.

#3 Feel real progress

Classes can feel like you are endlessly showing up and make vague progress. With a learning lab, you see the curriculum ahead of you and can check off one mini-lesson after the next.

#4 Mastery through teaching

Being exposed to material is one thing. Teaching it to another student develops a much deeper understanding.

#5 Make friends

Form meaningful friendships through in-depth learning interactions with peers.

Benefits For Educators

#1 Higher student retention

because students form friendships when they teach each other

#2 Students learn many times faster

Hands-on instruction, individual pacing, and frequent feedback of mentors adds up to a lot.

#3 Focus on higher level teaching

Let peer mentors handle the easy stuff. Focus on where your teaching expertise is really needed.

#4 Design the full learning journey

With drop-in classes, you have to focus on making a class for whoever shows up that day. With learning labs, everyone starts at the first lesson and can continue where they left of whenver they come back. That let's you focus on the whole student journey.

#5 Rapid iteration and refinement

The unit of a learning lab is two students and a 15-minute lesson. It's so quick and easy that it lets you test out lessons on students, incorprate their feedback, and iterate quickly.

Common Concerns

#1 "Why would anyone teach for free?"

Students quickly realize how much they learn from teaching and love the self esteem boost of being able to contribute to another student's learning. Plus, it's the pay-it-forwad model of receiving mentoring in exchange for mentoring.

#2 How do I know a peer mentor is teaching correctly?

Everyone has access to the lesson notes and can check the mentor. Plus, lessons are designed around a task or outcome. Successfully accomplishing that lesson goal is verification itself.

#3 I want to learn from a real teacher!

1:1 from a real teacher is ideal, but affordable from everyone. Peer mentors, with a well-structured curriculum, provide more guidance and feedback than a teacher in front of a large classes.

#5 Won’t this put me out of a job as a teacher?

Your role shifts from lecturing to guiding, creating curriculum, and supporting students as they teach. You gain more time to focus on higher-level aspects and more holistic student development.

Key Concepts to Understand

Learning Edge

Traditional classes have a single topic and fixed pace, leaving many only mildly engaged. In a learning lab each student pair has their own topic and pace, keeping each student right at the edge of their learning.

Asymmetric Learning

Students learn faster when taught by someone with mastery over a skill, avoiding the pitfalls of “beginners teaching beginners.”

"A person right in front of you"

A peer mentor can easily pick up on a learner’s struggles or confusion and adjust in real-time—no falling through the cracks.

Pointability

A clear curriculum lets students easily identify lessons they need, eliminating the "What should we do next?" dilemma.

Elastic Lessons

Lessons are adaptable, allowing mentors to keep it easy for struggling learners and dive deeper for faster learners (and lesson repetitions).

How To Get Started As A Student

  • Step #1: Show up
    Come to a learning lab (or find a mentor through the app).
  • Step #2: Learn
    Learn your first lesson from a peer mentor.
  • Step #3: Progress
    See your progress in the app.
  • Step #4: Teach
    Teach your first lesson to another new student. (The app helps you with teaching points.)
  • Step #5: Repeat
    Repeat step #2.

How To Get Started As An Educator

  • Step #1: Start with a small trial.
    Start with a workshop or class segment in your existing class (a curriculum of 1-4 mini-lessons).
  • Step #2: Create a visual breakdown of the subject
    Writing everything you want to teach on post-it notes and spreading them out on the floor is a great way to do that.
  • Step #3: Translate teaching topics into peer-mentoring friendly lessons.
    Mentors are bad at and don't enjoy regurgitating talking points. Make the core of a lesson a skill, ability, or outcome. Bad examples: List ten market segmentation dimensions, explain the Versailles Treaty. Good examples: Analyze your last coffee purchase for market positioning, show how to start a car on a hill.
  • Step #4: Enter your curriculum into the app
    (You don't have to do all up front. You can fill lessons in as the fastest student gets close.)
  • Step #5: Test and refine
    Test lessons with students, refine based on feedback, and iterate them quickly.
  • Step #6: Run your classes as learning labs.
    Pair students with the app. Track their progress in the app. Walk around the room to nurture your learning community.

How to design peer-mentor suitable mini-lessons

  • Less one-way lecture, more interactive
    Unlike traditional teaching, peer mentors thrive on hands-on, task-based lessons. Center a lesson around an ability, outcome, or exercise.
  • Small, concrete lessons
    Mentors are inspired when they have a small, concrete teaching task.
  • From information to abilities
    Shift from transferring information to students to developing abilities in students (e.g. critical analysis of historic sources).
  • Test on students
    It's hard to anticipate how students react to teaching lessons. Try them out, incorporate, and iterate. Expect to redo a lesson 2-3 times until it clicks with students.

Curriculum Overview

Our curriculum focuses on several key dimensions to transform you into a versatile dancer.

  1. Leading Technique: Beginning with basic lead signals and progressing to nuanced, complex conversational leading adjusted to your partner.
  2. Following Technique: Followers start by learning the basic rules, developing increasing responsivenes to create beautiful movements with styling injected.
  3. Movement Technique: Dancers start by developing body coordination, then learning hip, sensual movements, and so on. The study of movement quality is continues forever because it underpins the ability to lead/follow and connect with the music.
  4. Ear training/Musicality: Building a foundation of recognizing the beat, musical phases, and syncopation leads to higher level skills like matching the dance and music texture, underpinning the dance with contraction/relase
  5. Sensual Bachata Vocabulary: The vocabulary are the common patterns like the basics, types of turns, types of body waves, and so on.
  6. Choreography Development and Floorcraft: Specifically for leaders, this dimension hones the skill of crafting choreographies on the fly and adapting to the social dance floor's dynamic environment.

Neuroscience-Based Teaching

Since the publication of the Code of Terpsichore in 1830, dance has been taught in the way that you are most likely familiar with. The instructor wants a certain movement and tells you to move that way. You have probably realized that simply because someone logically explains you how to move can be far away from actually performing the move. Over the last two decades, neuroscience through rigorous studies and brain imaging developed an understanding about how we optimally learn movement/dance.

  1. Internal vs. external focus: Internal focus is when you think about the muscles and joints that you want to move. External focus is when you focus on the goal of your movement, your partner, or imagery. Think about driving down a windy mountain road. You can focus on your hands or get consumed in tracing the curves of the road. The latter will make your movements smooth, fluid, and precise because you allow the motor cortex to work out how to best employ your muscles.
  2. Explicit vs implicit learning: Explicit learning happens when instruction specifies all the details of the movement. Implicit learning happens when instruction specifies a strategic goal guiding the student to acquire the movement details correctly without thinking about them.
  3. Low vs. high bandwidth learning: Low bandwidth learning is repetition of the same movement. High bandwidth learning is variation of related movements. The motor cortext learns movement by making a plan and then evaluating the success. More variation provides the motor cortex with richer data to draw insights from.

The Learning Lab Teaching Format

Currently in the group class format: We are paving the way to develop a community of dancers at different levels. Once we’ve built that, we’ll convert to the learning lab format like we’ve done for Argentine Tango.

A structured approach: Unlike typical peer-to-peer learning experiences where peers are often unsure on what to do, the learning lab format is structured to be productive. The asymmetric pairing of a more beginning with a more advanced student means that the mentor takes charge and knows where to go. A curriculum designed for bite-sized, progressive mini-lessons provides a clear path forward.

Teaching as Learning: Why teach without compensation? It's simple - teaching is an integral part of your learning journey. Articulating concepts, answering questions, and addressing learners' challenges enriches your understanding and enhances self-esteem as you contribute to others' growth.

Addressing Accuracy Concerns: Worried about learning incorrect techniques? Our mini-lessons have clearly defined, tangible goals. Achieving these goals is a mutual indicator of successful learning. Moreover, both students and mentors have access to uniform teaching notes and resources through our companion app, ensuring consistency and accuracy in teaching.

Supported by a Dedicated App: To enhance the Learning Lab experience, our app houses the curriculum, lesson videos, and tracks your progress. It's a handy tool for communication and determining your next learning steps. Explore it here: learninglab.fans<
